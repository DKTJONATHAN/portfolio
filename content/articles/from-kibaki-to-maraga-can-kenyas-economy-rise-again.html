From Kibaki to Maraga: Can Kenya’s Economy Rise Again?

July 31, 2025 Business
MaragaKibakiRuto

.


As Kenya approaches the 2027 elections, the nation finds itself at a pivotal moment. Under President William Ruto, economic struggles high taxes, inflation, and public unrest have fueled widespread discontent. Into this fray steps David Maraga, the former Chief Justice, campaigning to “Reset, Restore, Rebuild” Kenya. His candidacy invites comparisons to Mwai Kibaki, the economist turned president who rescued Kenya’s economy after decades of mismanagement under Daniel arap Moi. This article dives into Kibaki’s economic playbook, explores Maraga’s potential to revive the economy, and weighs how their leadership styles could shape Kenya’s future.

Kibaki’s Economic Turnaround: A Lesson in “Kibakinomics”

When Mwai Kibaki became president in December 2002, Kenya was on its knees. Economic growth had stalled at 0.6%, corruption was bleeding the country dry, and international donors had turned their backs. Kibaki, with his First Class Honours Degree in Economics and years as Finance Minister, wasted no time. He rolled out a strategy dubbed “Kibakinomics,” blending fiscal discipline, privatization, and anti corruption efforts to breathe life back into the economy.

What Kibaki Did and How It Worked

  1. Tightened the Purse Strings: Kibaki cut wasteful government spending, reduced borrowing, and boosted tax collection. This steadied the economy and won back investors.
  2. Privatization Push: He sold off state owned companies and launched public offerings, pumping money into the economy without piling on debt.
  3. Cracking Down on Corruption: Kibaki fired 400 procurement officers and pushed transparency, clawing back resources lost to graft.
  4. Vision 2030: This long term plan, crafted with experts like Dr. Wahome Gakuru, prioritized infrastructure and jobs, laying a foundation for growth.
  5. Smart Balance: Kibaki mixed free market ideas with strategic government moves like the stimulus package after the 2007/2008 election chaos to keep the economy humming.

The results were stunning. By the end of his first term, Kenya’s GDP growth hit 7.1%, earning praise as an African success story. Kenyans remember those years for better roads, more jobs, and a sense of progress. But it wasn’t flawless. Kibaki stumbled politically, failing to deliver a new constitution as promised and facing criticism after a 2005 draft was rejected. Still, his economic wins remain a benchmark.

David Maraga isn’t an economist he’s a judge with a reputation for integrity. As Chief Justice, he made history by nullifying the 2017 presidential election, cementing his image as a champion of fairness. Now, as a 2027 presidential hopeful, he’s pitching himself as the antidote to Kenya’s current woes, blaming “governance failures” for the nation’s slide toward a “failed state.”

Today’s Economic Mess Under Ruto

Kenya in 2025 is a tough place:

  1. Tax Hikes and Inflation: Heavy taxes and rising prices have 50.5% of Kenyans saying life’s gotten worse (Afrobarometer, 2024).
  2. Street Anger: Protests against economic mismanagement have left over 100 dead in the last year (BBC News, July 2025).
  3. Debt Trouble: A fiscal deficit of 4.3% of GDP looms, with debt payments eating up funds and aid drying up.

Maraga’s campaign leans hard on rule of law and transparency. He argues that fixing corruption and rebuilding trust are the first steps to fixing the economy. But can a judge without Kibaki’s economic chops pull it off?

Kibaki vs. Maraga: Economics Meets Governance. Kibaki and Maraga both hate corruption, but their toolkits couldn’t be more different. Here’s how they stack up:


Kibaki’s Track Record:

  1. Economic Know How: Kibaki’s training and experience let him craft policies that delivered growth fast.
  2. Real Results: From 0.6% to 7.1% GDP growth, his impact was measurable and felt by millions.
  3. Team Player: He leaned on experts to turn his vision into reality.

Maraga's Promise:

  1. Governance First: Maraga’s all about strong institutions and accountability crucial for economic stability, but less direct than Kibaki’s approach.
  2. Corruption Fighter: His judicial backbone could stop the rot that’s holding Kenya back.
  3. The Catch: He’s no economist. To succeed, he’d need a crack team of advisors think Kibaki style brain trust.
  4. Kibaki built the house; Maraga wants to fix the foundation. One delivered jobs and roads, the other could deliver trust and order.


Maraga’s Playbook: How He Could Turn Things Around. If Maraga wins in 2027, here’s how he might tackle Kenya’s economic crisis:

  1. Stronger Institutions: Independent courts and agencies could lure investors by promising fairness.
  2. Corruption Cleanup: Using his judicial know how, he could save billions lost to graft for schools, hospitals, and roads.
  3. Tax Relief: Cutting taxes on basics could ease the squeeze on families and spark spending.
  4. People First Policies: Focusing on inclusive growth might calm protests and rebuild faith in government.
  5. The big “if”? Maraga would need top notch economic advisors to turn his governance wins into jobs and growth. Without them, his vision could stall.


The Bottom Line: Two Roads to Recovery


Kibaki and Maraga offer Kenya different flavors of hope. Kibaki’s legacy is a roaring economy with some political dents proof that expertise can deliver. Maraga’s potential is a cleaner, fairer system that could set the stage for prosperity, if he plays his cards right. Post Ruto, Kenya needs both: Kibaki’s economic savvy and Maraga’s moral compass. Whether Maraga can blend the two or find his own path will decide if Kenya’s economy rises again. History says it’s possible. The future’s up to him.
