NACADA Raises the Bar: Legal Drinking Age Now 21 Amidst Shisha Ban Controversy

July 30, 2025 News
NACADAAlcoholShishaDrugs

The decision to increase the legal drinking age reflects mounting concerns over the rising potency of alcoholic substances and their devastating effects on public health.


Nairobi, Kenya, On July 30, 2025, the National Campaign Against Alcohol and Drug Abuse (NACADA) launched its 2025 National Alcohol Policy, introducing sweeping changes to address the growing issue of substance abuse in Kenya. The policy, unveiled by Interior Cabinet Secretary Murkomen, raises the legal drinking age from 18 to 21, marking a significant shift in the country’s regulatory framework. Alongside this headline measure, NACADA has rolled out additional restrictions aimed at curbing alcohol and drug misuse, particularly among the nation’s youth.

The decision to increase the legal drinking age reflects mounting concerns over the rising potency of alcoholic substances and their devastating effects on public health. The new policy also prohibits alcohol sales in public spaces such as sports facilities, medical centers, supermarkets, and restaurants. Furthermore, it bans online alcohol orders and home deliveries while emphasizing stricter oversight of digital platforms to prevent the promotion and sale of drugs to minors.

Key Measures in the 2025 National Alcohol Policy 

  1. Legal Drinking Age: Raised from 18 to 21. 
  2. Alcohol-Free Zones: No sales permitted near schools, churches, or other designated public spaces. 
  3. Minimum Package Size: Alcohol must be sold in containers of at least 250ml. 
  4. Outlet Limits: The number of alcohol retailers will be restricted based on population density. 
  5. Online Regulation: on online alcohol sales and increased monitoring of drug-related content.

NACADA’s latest efforts signal a pivot toward prevention and recovery, engaging a “whole-of-society” approach to tackle substance abuse. However, the policy’s launch has reignited debate over the agency’s ability to enforce its directives, with critics pointing to a lingering controversy surrounding the sale of Shisha.

Shisha Ban: A Policy Undermined

Previously, NACADA imposed a nationwide ban on Shisha, a flavored tobacco product popular in social settings. Despite this prohibition, Shisha continues to be openly sold in entertainment joints across Kenya, with little apparent enforcement. Patrons and proprietors alike report that the product remains widely available, raising doubts about NACADA’s capacity to implement its regulations effectively. “The Shisha ban was clear, but you can still find it everywhere,” noted a Nairobi resident. “What’s to say these new rules won’t go the same way?” 

This enforcement gap has fueled skepticism about the 2025 policy’s prospects. Observers question whether the raised drinking age and other measures will face similar challenges, potentially driving alcohol sales underground rather than reducing consumption.

Public Response: Support and Skepticis

Reactions to the new policy are divided. Some Kenyans welcome the focus on protecting youth, with one parent stating, “Raising the drinking age to 21 could save lives.” Others, however, express concern over the economic fallout for businesses reliant on alcohol sales. “Enforcement is the real test,” said a bar owner in Nairobi. “Without it, this is just noise.” Social media platforms buzz with similar sentiments, blending cautious optimism with calls for accountability.

As Kenya steps into this new phase of its battle against substance abuse, the success of NACADA’s 2025 National Alcohol Policy hinges on effective implementation. With the legal drinking age now set at 21 and a slate of ambitious regulations in place, the agency faces the dual challenge of changing behavior and restoring public trust. Whether these measures will curb alcohol and drug abuse or mirror the unresolved Shisha ban remains a question only time will answer.
