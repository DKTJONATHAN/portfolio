REVEALED: Kenya Ranked First On Watchlist For Severe Human Rights Violations

August 2, 2025 News
Human RightsCIVICUSKenya

Kenya has emerged as a troubling leader on the CIVICUS Monitor Watchlist, its civic space downgraded to "Repressed" a severe classification that places it among 51 nations facing significant curtailments of fundamental rights. This shift from its "Obstructed" rating in 2023 signals a dramatic decline in democratic freedoms, driven by a government crackdown on protests, digital expression, and human rights defenders. Once hailed as a beacon of stability in East Africa, Kenya now grapples with a repressive environment that threatens its democratic fabric, raising urgent questions about the balance between security and civic liberties.

The Spark Violent Protest Crackdowns

The catalyst for Kenya's watchlist prominence was the government's brutal response to protests in June 2025, marking the one-year anniversary of the RejectFinanceBill movement. Kenyans took to the streets to demand economic reforms and accountability, but the response was anything but peaceful. Authorities deployed excessive force, including tear gas, rubber bullets, and live ammunition, resulting in at least 65 deaths and over 1500 arrests. Charges ranging from murder to terrorism have been leveled against demonstrators, a move critics argue is designed to criminalize dissent. The youngest victim, 12-year-old Kennedy Onyango, killed by gunfire in Kajiado County, epitomizes the human cost of this crackdown.

Compounding the violence, unmarked security forces and suspected state-sponsored goons infiltrated protests, attacking demonstrators and looting businesses despite a High Court order in June 2025 banning such tactics. The Kenya National Commission on Human Rights documented hundreds of injuries and cases of rape, including gang assaults, painting a picture of systemic repression. CIVICUS has labeled this response a "perilous threshold," suggesting Kenya's government is systematically dismantling civic engagement.

Digital Repression A Silent War Online

Beyond the streets, Kenya's digital landscape has become a battleground for freedom. The death of activist Albert Omondi Ojwang, who succumbed to torture in police custody over a social media post, shocked the nation. His case, confirmed by autopsy as strangulation, underscores the lethal risks of online dissent. Similarly, software developer Rose Njeri's arrest under the 2018 Misuse of Computer and Cybercrimes Act for creating a digital tool highlights the criminalization of tech activism.

Censorship has extended to creative expression, with protest-themed art and programs banned in public spaces and schools. The government blocked BBC films documenting police shootings and issued court orders against controversial plays, stifling cultural resistance. Proposed legislation, including the Communications Bill dubbed a "Trojan horse for mass surveillance" and a Ksh150 million budget increase for the Directorate of Criminal Investigations to track social media users, threaten to entrench digital authoritarianism. A January 2025 directive mandating social media companies to establish local offices further raises concerns about regulatory overreach.

Targeting Human Rights Defenders

The repression extends to human rights defenders and civil society organizations, who face heightened surveillance and harassment. Martin Mavenjina, a Ugandan senior adviser at the Kenya Human Rights Commission, was deported on July 6, 2025, despite valid permits, narrating a harrowing ordeal of detention and intimidation. KHRC Executive Director Davis Malombe faced similar pressure, and on the same date, state-sponsored goons disrupted a KHRC press conference, underscoring the hostile environment for advocacy.

This targeting reflects a broader pattern of intimidation, abduction, and judicial harassment. The arbitrary arrest of human rights defenders like Mark Amiani, Mulingwa Nzau, and Francis Mutunge Mwangi in Mombasa, coupled with attacks on women defenders at a Nairobi press conference, illustrate a systematic effort to silence dissent.

Global Context and Implications

Kenya's "Repressed" rating aligns it with nations like the United States, Turkey, and El Salvador on the July 2025 watchlist, reflecting a global trend of democratic backsliding. CIVICUS data indicates that civic freedoms are nearing the "Closed" category, the worst classification, where rights are virtually nonexistent. This deterioration since 2023 when initial concerns centered on election-related repression suggests a deepening crisis.

A Crossroads for Kenya

As Kenya leads this watchlist, it stands at a critical juncture. International watchdogs call for accountability, urging the government to reverse course and protect civic rights. Yet, with proposed laws and ongoing crackdowns, the nation risks sliding further into repression. Will Kenya reclaim its democratic promise, or will it cement its place as a leader in civic freedom suppression? The answer lies in the balance it strikes in the months ahead.
